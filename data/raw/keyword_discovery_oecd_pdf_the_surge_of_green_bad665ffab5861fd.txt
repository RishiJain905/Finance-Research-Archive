The share of GSSS bonds in total bond issuance from LAC in international markets surged from 9.3% in 2020 to nearly 35% in 2023 . Over the past decade, GSSS bonds in LAC have accumulated USD 131 billion, with green bonds nearly doubling from USD 18.8 billion in 2019 to USD 36 billion in 2023. Social, sustainability and sustainability -linked bonds (SLBs) have also grown substantially, reaching USD 95.1 billion by 2023. Sovereign GSSS bonds in international markets increased from 36% of total sovereign issuance in 2022 to 5 0% in 2023, while conventional sovereign bonds dropped from 64% to 50%. Among GSSS bonds, SLBs dominated, representing 36% of GSSS bond issuance in 2023. Chile and Uruguay are the leading issuers of sovereign SLBs, as they are currently the only two countries globally to have issued such bonds. The international GSSS bond market in LAC has diversified its purpose from traditional sectors like energy and food to include automotive, technology and real estate. Gender bonds and digital transformation initiatives are also emerging trends in LAC. LAC countries are making notable progress in the development of sustainable finance framework s. By December 2023, 14 countries had initiated nearly 221 actions, led by Brazil, Colombia and Mexico. This includes actions by governments, private actors and multilateral organisations. Guidelines for GSSS bond issuance, green protocols and taxonomies, along with the creation of green committees have been initiated and developed. However, if GSSS are to become a more significant and used source of financing, aligned with the SDGs and the Paris Agreement objectives, further development is needed to refine these frameworks , boost investor confidence, and foster cross-border harmonisation. It is necessary to differentiate and strengthen the regulatory and supervisory mechanisms for SLBs compared to GSS bonds, as SLBs rely on self -established key performance indicators (KPIs) and sustainability performance targets (SPTs). Collaboration between ministries and public investment offices is also essential to identify sustainable, bankable projects and ensure efficient use of resources . These projects should be supported by robust data and metrics for accurate assessment. Additionally, GSSS bonds in LAC should operate under more robust regulatory frameworks to align financing with national develop ment goals, integrating economic, social, governance (E SG) considerations, managing climate risks, and supporting sustainability financing. Finally, i t is crucial to continue enhancing sustainable finance frameworks to improve comparability, credibility and transparency at both national and regional levels, through improved co-ordination of multiple stakeholders across sectors at national, regional and international levels. International guidelines, such as the International Capital Markets Association (ICMA) principles and the EU Taxonomy, and the regional framework developed by the UN Working Group on Taxonomies of Sustainable Finance in L AC provide direction for GSSS bond issuance in the region, but clearer more binding standards, better external reviews, and greater data transparency are still required to element s (i.e. the pandemic, the growing impact of climate change and social justice movements) and favourable market conditions (i.e. low global interest rates until 2022 and increased investor interest) (ECLAC, 2022 [1]).There has been rapid growth in demand from investors and global institutions for ESG-linked investment opportunities. Green, social, sustainability, and sustainability-linked (GSSS) bonds have gained prominence as a key asset class within the wider univers e of ESG assets. European funds’ willingness to take a longer -term view of risk has been one of the drivers of the rise of impact investing through GSSS bonds (OECD et al., 2023 [2]). GSSS bonds are financial instruments designed to fund projects with positive environmental and social impacts or, in the case of SLBs, to achieve sustainability targets that are not directly tied to specific projects . These bonds attract both public and private investments towards projects that can drive economic growth, improve social equity, and promote environmental sustainability in emerging economies. In the LAC region, GSSS bonds represent a unique opportunity to link the need for greater financing , including debt , to sustainability criteria. By allowing greater financing of initiatives such as renewable energy projects, social infrastructure, and sustainable agriculture, GSSS bonds can help LAC countries achieve their development goals in a more sustainable and inclusi ve manner. For sovereigns in LAC, raising funds through these types of assets has become an attractive option to increase the return on liquid global capital, diversify the investor base, mobilise direct capital into sustainable activities, and acquire financial support for creating sustainable capital markets. Bond proceeds can fund sustainable public expenditure and infrastructure, as well as sustainable policies and national commitments, such as the Nationally Determined Contributions (NDC) set under the Paris Agreement on climate change (OECD et al., 2023[2]). For corporates in LAC, issuing GSSS bonds in international markets is attractive as it can offer lower interest rates and greater potential for capital gains. Bond proceeds can also benefit sectoral investments, such as the agricultural sector (agribusiness, food and beverages, forestry), the financial sector (commercial banks, financial servic es companies, finance development banks/multilateral agencies), and the energy sector (utilities, oil, gas, power) There are two types of structures in the ESG sustainable debt market: use of proceeds and target -linked (Figure 1). According to t he International Capital Market Association (ICMA) , green, social and sustainability bonds belong to the first type of structure. These are fixed -income instruments whose proceeds are exclusively applied to finance or refinance, entirely or partially, environmental and social projects or a combination of both. Sustainability-linked (SLBs) differ from green, social and sustainability bonds in that they are target -linked and allow financing outside of specific projects or use of proceeds categories. SLBs are tracked through the assessment of KPI s and SPTs . For SLBs, the issuers choose the associated targets they want to achieve through the issuance of the bond, and the structural an d/or financial characteristics of the bond will change depending on whether the pre -determined target is achieved or not. 1 Introduction  9 THE SURGE OF GREEN, SOCIAL, SUSTAINABILITY AND SUSTAINABILITY-LINKED (GSSS) BONDS IN LATIN AMERICA AND THE CARIBBEAN © OECD 2024 Figure 1. The surge in GSSS bond issuance in the LAC region brings forth several, interconnected challenges. They range from issues related to market maturity and capacity building to developing solid regulatory frameworks that promote transparency and reporting. This paper focuses on the latter aspects, underscoring the importance for countries in the region to enhance their sustainable finance frameworks. Regulatory frameworks are essential for monitoring and verifying the issuance of GSSS bonds. The design and implementation of robust reporting and impact measurement standards can be challenging but it is necessary to maintain investor confidence. Countries in LAC are focusing their efforts on expanding and improving their sustainable finance frameworks. These frameworks include protocols, international co -operation initiatives, standards, guidelines and taxonomies that aim to reduce and manage the ESG risks of financial activities while encouraging the flow of capital to assets, projects, sectors, and companies with environmental, climate, and social benefits. The paper is organised as follows. F irst, it provides an assessment of GSSS bond issu ances in the LAC region, emphasising how the surge has been accompanied by a parallel increase in sovereign bonds and innovative structures for SLBs. It is still considered a niche sector, representing only 1% of total assets outstanding and around 2% of new issuances globally (OECD, 2022 [3]). In the LAC region, however, GSSS bonds are proving to be an attractive financing mechanism, increasing from 9.3% of total LAC bond issuance in international markets in 2020 to almost 35% in 2023. In LAC, raising funds through these instruments instead of traditional bonds has become an attractive option for increasing returns on liquid global capital , diversifying the investor base , mobilising direct capital into sustainable activities, and acquiring financial support for creating sustainable capital markets. Between 2014 and 2023, the GSSS international bond market in LAC reached a cumulative value of USD 131 billion (Figure 2, Panel A). Total green bond issuance in LAC nearly doubled over four years, from USD 18.8 billion in 2019 to USD 36 billion in 2023. The growth was even more pronounced for social, sustainability, and sustainability-linked bonds, reaching a cumulative issuance of USD 95.1 billion in 2023 since the inception of this market segment in 2016 (OECD et al., 2023 [2]). SLBs are innovative, forward-looking financial instruments distinguished by their dynamic structural and/or financial characteristics (OECD, 2024[7]; OECD et al., 2023[2]). These characteristics are contingent on the issuers meeting pre-determined KPIs and SPTs. In contrast to GSS bonds, SLBs can offer potential rewards, such as lower coupon ra tes for meeting sustainability goals, or penalties for falling short. SLBs offer financing solutions in areas such as biodiversity, climate change adaptation and social development – areas that GSS bonds often cannot target due to a lack of large, bankable asset pipelines (OECD, 2024[7]). Unlike GSS bonds, which need specific assets, SLBs can be backed by an issuer’s entire balance sheet, enabling higher values and longer maturities (OECD, 2024[7]). SLBs innovative structures in LAC are financing key development areas with multiple sustainable objectives. The share of SLBs in total GSSS bond issuance stood at 36% in 2023, with sovereigns leading the issuance. Despite high volatility in re cent years, SLB issuance in developing countries grew significantly following the first issuance in 2019 (OECD, 2024[7]). Yet issuances from developing countries, which have the greatest financing needs, account for a small share of the total SLB market – and appear to be decreasing. Between 2021 and 2022, the share of SLBs from issuers in ODA -eligible countries as a percentage of the total issued amount decreased from 13% to 5% (OECD, 2024[7]). Sovereign SLBs can be linked to existing sustainability commitments, enhancing their credibility and relevance. These instruments can serve as tools to assist the national government in fulfilling its obligations under its NDC and the Paris Agreement. They can facilitate the transition of industries traditionally viewed as “dirty”, which are often more challenging to finance through use-of-proceeds instruments like GSS. With this issuance, Chile became the first country in the world to link its bond issuance to bot h gender equality and its NDC commitment on climate change (OECD, 2024[7]). To scale up SLBs in the region, international financial co -operation is crucial for overcoming barriers hindering market development, particularly the establishment of reliable monitoring and supervision mechanisms to prevent green-/SDG-washing. At present, SLBs are at high risk of such washing due to the lack of uniform sectoral principles and guidelines, immaterial or unambitious KPIs and SPTs, insufficient data quality and a shortage of skilled analysts to assess KPIs. International co -operation can expand the availability of data and indicators to track sustainab ility progress. Regulation and monitoring need improvement, and definitions should be harmonised regionally and internationally to enhance market transparency (see below on sustainable finance frameworks). Additionally, international capacity -building partnerships are essential to bridge regulatory and co -ordination gaps. Developing and utilising locally adapted SLB guidelines that prioritise sustainability will be crucial for developing local -currency bond markets, mitigating currency risk and enhancing financial resilience (OECD, 2024[7]). 16  THE SURGE OF GREEN, SOCIAL, SUSTAINABILITY AND SUSTAINABILITY-LINKED (GSSS) BONDS IN LATIN AMERICA AND THE CARIBBEAN © OECD 2024 Sustainable finance frameworks are tailored roadmaps aligning funding with a country ’s development goals. These frameworks rely on various mechanisms such as taxonomies, standards guidelines, policies, international co -operation initiatives, and regulatory requirements, developed by the public or pr ivate sector, or both, to guide the issuance of GSSS bonds and other sustainable instruments at the international, regional and national levels. Frameworks often follow three key objectives: ESG integration in financial sector operations; climate risk management; and financing sustainability. Frameworks’ diverse components make a unified definition challenging, requiring enhanced co-ordination. Greater efforts in the region are needed to foster more consolidated sustainable finance frameworks, ensuring comp arability, certainty, credibility, integrity and transparency in the market. Sustainable finance frameworks guide the issuance of GSSS bonds at international, regional and national levels (Figure 4). At the international level, the principles of the ICMA and the Helsinki Principles are often used as overarching guiding frameworks for the issuance of GSSS bonds by LAC countries, both regionally and nationally (OECD et al., 2023 [2]). ICMA’s suite of instruments – Green Bond Principles, Social Bond Principles, Sustainabili ty Bond Guidelines and Sustainability -Linked Bond Principles – have become increasingly important in international markets. Most countries and corporates around the world now use them as reference to develop their own frameworks for the issuance of GSSS bonds (OECD et al., 2023[2]). At the regional level, the EU Sustainable Finance Framework , which includes the EU Green Bond Standard, and the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) green and social bond standards are also employed as guiding references for the issuance of GSSS and other sustainable finance mechanisms. Sustainable finance frameworks for GSS bonds differ from that of SLBs. For use -of-proceeds or GSS bonds, standards and taxonomies have been developed to guide issuance, certification and verification (Figure 4, Panel A). For target -linked bonds or SLBs, standards have also been developed to guide issuance, certificati on and verification. In addition, the final step involves evaluating issuer level “self-imposed” KPIs and SPTs ( Figure 4, Panel B). When examining sustainable finance frameworks in LAC countries, green and sustainable taxonomies stand out. These initiatives have increasingly been adopted throughout the region since 2022 (Table 3). However, this remains one of the areas that requires further development and refinement by most countries in the region. As of May 2024, they had been published in Argentina, Chile, Colombia, the Dominican Republic, Mexico and Panama, and were under development in countries such as Brazil , Costa Rica and Peru ( Table 3). These taxonomies establish technical assessment criteria and define green, social, sustainability, or sustainability -linked activities based on prioritised s ectors and sustainable development goals. While taxonomies can inform the selection of KPIs and SPTs for SLBs, they are more central to GSS bonds, which rely on them to define eligible projects for the use of proceeds. Taxonomies enhance the credibility of these instruments and help counter green-/SDG-washing. In April 2022, the Colombian government published the region ’s first green taxonomy with a focus on land -use sectors. Since then, seven more countries have published or made significant progress in de veloping sustainable or green taxonomies, focusing on key sectors such as climate change mitigation and adaptation, the circular economy, biodiversity protection, land use, electricity, and water. Encouraging financial institutions to report to regulators or industry associations on the outcomes of GSSS loans, bonds, and other instruments remains crucial.
